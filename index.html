Conflict Learning Sequence

How to use these two conflict activities

Please complete the activities in order. The first activity helps you notice your usual instinct in conflict. The second activity helps you learn when a different response may be more effective.

Why this sequence matters

Most people have a default reaction in conflict. Some people push harder. Some avoid. Some give in too quickly. Some try to smooth things over. That default is not automatically wrong. In some situations, it may help. In other situations, it may cost you clarity, trust, fairness, or results.

The goal of these activities is not to judge your personality. The goal is to help you recognize your pattern and then learn how to choose a response that better fits the situation.

Step 1. Start with Activity 1: Conflict Style Finder 🔎

This first activity is a self assessment. You will work through realistic conflict situations and choose what you would most likely do first.

Important: at this stage, you are not trying to pick the “best” answer. You are trying to be honest about your instinctive pattern.

Step 2. Read your result carefully 🧠

At the end of Activity 1, you will see a profile showing the pattern that appeared most often in your choices.

Treat that result as a starting point for self awareness. It is not a formal diagnosis. It is simply a way to help you notice what you tend to do under pressure.

Step 3. Then move to Activity 2: Conflict Styles Activity ↔️

This second activity teaches the actual conflict style framework more directly. Here, the goal changes.

Now you are no longer asking, “What do I usually do?” You are asking, “What response fits this situation best?”

Step 4. Pay attention to situational fit 🎯

As you work through Activity 2, notice that the best response depends on things like urgency, relationship stakes, emotional heat, and accountability.

This is the key idea of the unit: effective conflict handling is situational. A style that works beautifully in one context may be weak or even harmful in another.

Step 5. Notice the gap between your default and the best fit ✍️

The most important learning may happen when your instinct and the strongest strategy are not the same.

That gap is where growth happens. If you notice, for example, that your default is to accommodate or avoid, but the situation calls for clearer accountability or more direct collaboration, that awareness helps you pause and choose more intentionally.

What I want you to take from this

Do not reduce yourself to a label. The point is not to say, “This is just who I am.” The point is to understand your most likely pattern so that you can recognize it in real time and decide whether it is actually serving the moment.

In other words, your default may be familiar and comfortable. But the most effective choice may require you to stretch beyond that default.

As you complete Activity 1
  • Answer honestly based on what you would most likely do first.
  • Do not try to outguess the activity.
  • Notice repeated patterns in your choices.
  • Read your result as self awareness, not judgment.
As you complete Activity 2
  • Move through the sections in order.
  • Read the context before choosing.
  • Focus on fit, not on what sounds nicest.
  • Use the reflection to identify where you may need to stretch.
Suggested reflection prompt

After completing both activities, think about this question:

What seems to be your default response in conflict, and in what kind of situation might that default help you or hurt you?
